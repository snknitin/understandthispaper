Reinforcement learning (RL) has become a central component of post-training large language models (LLMs), yet little is understood about how RL adaptation is distributed across transformer layers. Existing approaches typically update all model parameters uniformly, implicitly assuming that every layer contributes similarly to the gains obtained during RL post-training. The authors challenge this assumption through a systematic layer-wise study of RL training and find that training a single transformer layer can recover most of the gains achieved by full-parameter RL training, and in some cases even surpass it. To quantify this phenomenon they introduce the 'layer contribution' metric, C(k). Across seven models spanning two model families (Qwen3, Qwen2.5), three RL algorithms (GRPO, GiGPO, Dr. GRPO), and multiple task domains, RL gains are highly concentrated in a small subset of, and in many cases even a single, transformer layer -- typically located in the middle of the stack.

Plain EnglishFor each prompt, the model generates G candidate answers, and each gets a binary reward for correctness. Instead of learning a separate 'critic' network to judge how good each answer is, GRPO grades each answer against its own siblings: take this answer's reward, subtract the group's average reward, and divide by the group's standard deviation. The result, A-hat_i, is positive if the answer beat its groupmates and negative if it lagged them, expressed in comparable units across prompts of very different difficulty.

What each color means

Advantage of response i

The quantity this equation defines: a per-response score saying how much better response i did than its groupmates. It replaces the learned value network that other RL algorithms (like PPO) need.

This response's reward

The raw reward for response i: a binary correct/incorrect signal from an automatic answer checker (verifiable reward).

Group average (baseline)

The average reward across all G responses sampled for the same prompt. Subtracting it centers the score: only doing better than your groupmates counts as positive.

Group spread (normalizer)

The standard deviation of the group's rewards. Dividing by it rescales the advantage so prompts where responses vary wildly and prompts where they barely differ produce comparable learning signals.

IntuitionIt's grading on a curve. A student's raw exam score (the reward) means little on its own -- what matters is how far above or below the class average they landed (subtract the mean), measured in units of how spread out the class was (divide by the std). An 80 in a class averaging 50 is a triumph; the same 80 in a class averaging 95 is a warning sign. Each group of G sampled responses is the 'class' for that prompt.

hover or click any colored piece — or a card on the right
Plain EnglishThis is the score that RL training maximizes. For each prompt, average over the G sampled responses; for each response, multiply how much likelier the new policy makes it (the ratio rho_i) by how good it was relative to its group (the advantage from Eq. 1). To keep updates stable, the ratio is also computed in a clipped form and the smaller (more pessimistic) of the two is used, so no single response can yank the policy too far in one step. Finally, a KL penalty, weighted by beta, is subtracted to keep the trained model from drifting too far from a frozen reference model. The paper's central move is that this objective is held fixed while only the set of trainable parameters changes: full-parameter training updates all of theta, single-layer training updates just one layer.

What each color means

The objective being maximized

The single scalar the whole RL training run pushes uphill on. Both full-parameter training and the paper's single-layer training maximize exactly this -- they differ only in which parameters get updated.

Average over prompts & samples

Expectation over training prompts x and the groups of responses {y_i} sampled for each -- in practice, an average over mini-batches of prompts and their sampled completions.

Average over the group

Each of the G sampled responses contributes one term; averaging makes the objective independent of the group size.

Unclipped policy-gradient term

The raw update signal: the importance ratio rho_i (how much more likely the current policy makes this response than the old one) times its advantage A-hat_i from Eq. (1).

Clipped safety version

The same term but with the ratio clamped to [1-eps, 1+eps]. Taking the min of the clipped and unclipped versions stops the policy from moving too aggressively on any single response.

KL leash to the reference model

A penalty (weighted by beta) on how far the trained policy drifts from a frozen reference policy, preventing the model from forgetting its pretrained behavior while chasing reward.

IntuitionThink of coaching a debate team after a scrimmage. Each debater (response) gets a grade relative to teammates (the advantage). You adjust your coaching emphasis toward what the high scorers did (the ratio-times-advantage term), but with a house rule: no single scrimmage can change your coaching by more than a fixed amount (the clip), and you must stay recognizably close to the playbook you started the season with (the KL leash to the reference model).

Plain EnglishThis is ordinary gradient ascent on the GRPO objective with one restriction: only the weights of a single chosen transformer layer, layer k, are allowed to move; every other parameter -- embeddings, the language model head, and all remaining layers -- stays exactly at its pretrained value. The gradient itself is still computed by backpropagating through the whole network, so it accounts for how all the frozen layers process information; the freeze only applies to the update step. Repeating this experiment once per layer, for every layer in the model, produces the per-layer scores S_k that feed the layer contribution metric C(k) in Eq. (4).

What each color means

Only layer k gets updated

The assignment arrow: layer k's weights are overwritten with their new value. No other weights appear on the left-hand side -- this is the entire experimental intervention of the paper.

Step size (learning rate)

How far to step along the gradient. Crucially, it is the same learning rate that was tuned for the full-parameter baseline, reused for every single-layer run to keep the comparison fair.

Gradient through the full network

The slope of the GRPO objective with respect to layer k's weights. Backpropagation still flows through every layer -- the gradient 'sees' the whole model -- but only layer k's slice of it is used.

Everything else frozen

Embeddings, the LM head, and all other transformer layers keep their pretrained values (requires_grad=False in PyTorch). This isolation is what lets the paper attribute any performance gain to layer k alone.

IntuitionImagine a relay team where you're only allowed to coach one runner. The team still runs the full race every time (backprop through the whole network), and you watch the complete race to decide your feedback (the gradient depends on all layers) -- but only your assigned runner gets to adjust their technique (only theta_k updates); everyone else must run exactly as before (frozen). By coaching each runner in separate seasons and comparing team times, you learn which single runner's improvement matters most.

\[\class{eqspan-e3-ck}{\mathcal{C}(k)}=\frac{\class{eqspan-e3-numerator}{S_{k}-S_{\text{base}}}}{\class{eqspan-e3-denominator}{S_{\text{full}}-S_{\text{base}}}}\]

Plain EnglishThis equation defines a single number, C(k), that tells you what percentage of the total benefit of RL fine-tuning you get if you only unfreeze and train one transformer layer, layer k, instead of the whole model. The numerator, S_k minus S_base, is the raw improvement that single-layer training buys you over the untrained baseline. The denominator, S_full minus S_base, is the raw improvement that ordinary full-parameter RL training buys you over that same baseline. Dividing the two normalizes single-layer training against the best case (updating everything), so C(k) = 1.0 means training just layer k recovers all of full-parameter RL's benefit, values above 1.0 mean it does even better, and values near 0 mean that layer barely helps at all.

What each color means

Layer contribution

The score this equation defines: how much of the full RL improvement one isolated layer k can capture on its own, expressed as a fraction/ratio.

Single-layer gain (numerator)

The improvement that training only layer k buys you: its benchmark score S_k minus the untrained baseline score S_base. This isolates the RL-induced gain from training just one layer.

Full-parameter RL gain (denominator)

The total improvement achieved when every layer in the model is updated by standard full-parameter RL -- the normalizing reference. Dividing by this turns the single-layer gain into a fraction of the best-case full-model gain.

IntuitionImagine a car's overall speedup after a full engine tune-up (S_full versus its stock performance S_base). Now imagine only replacing one component -- say, just the spark plugs -- and measuring the speedup that gives (S_k versus S_base). Layer contribution C(k) is the ratio of the single-part upgrade's speedup to the full tune-up's speedup: if replacing just the spark plugs gets you 90% of the full tune-up's benefit, that part had a layer contribution of 0.9, telling you it was doing most of the real work all along.
