Paper & source

FAIR (Meta) + NYU study that trains a single decoder-only transformer from scratch on text and vision together (Transfusion-style), instead of bolting vision onto a pretrained LLM. Key findings: one semantic visual representation (RAE) suffices for both understanding and generation; vision and language data are complementary; world modeling emerges from general pretraining; MoE scales multimodal training efficiently.

Plain EnglishOne transformer is graded by two teachers at once. The language teacher scores how well it predicts the next word in text (blue). The vision teacher hands it a partially-smudged image latent and scores how accurately it points back toward the clean image (purple prediction vs. red truth). Two knobs (amber, teal) set how much each grade counts. Training on both simultaneously — rather than starting from a finished LLM — is the paper's core experimental choice.

What each color means

Text loss weight

How much the language grade counts. The paper pins this to 1.0 and tunes the vision weight around it.

Language modeling loss (Eq. 1)

Standard next-token prediction on text: for every position, how surprised was the model by the actual next token? Identical to how GPT-style LLMs are trained.

Vision loss weight

The knob balancing the vision objective against text, 'chosen to stabilize joint training'. Non-trivial because the paper finds vision is far more data-hungry than language.

Average over noise & time

The vision loss is averaged over clean latents from the frozen encoder (z0), Gaussian noise draws (epsilon), and random interpolation times t — the model trains at every level of 'smudge'.

Predicted velocity (Eq. 2)

Given a partially-noised latent z_t, the time t, and the multimodal context, the same transformer predicts which direction to move the latent to make it a real image.

True velocity (target)

The straight-line direction from noise (epsilon) to the clean visual latent (z0) — the ground-truth answer the prediction is regressed against.

IntuitionA student enrolled in two classes with one brain (theta): a writing class — guess the next word of a passage — and a restoration class — shown a half-cleaned painting (z_t) and the clock (t), say which way each brushstroke should move (v_theta), graded against the restorer's true stroke (z0 − epsilon). The two lambdas are how much each class counts toward the final GPA. The paper's finding: taking both classes together makes you better at each than cramming either alone.
